Test Cricket in the T20 Era: Still Relevant, Still Supreme

Lord's Cricket Ground, London — the spiritual home of Test cricket.

Cricket March 2026 ⏱ 8 min read

There is a moment in Test cricket that no other format can manufacture.

It arrives somewhere around the third afternoon of a five-day match. The batting side has been ground down session by session. The pitch is crumbling at the edges and the ball is reversing sharply. A fast bowler marks out his run-up for the forty-seventh time that day. The batsman at the crease has occupied his ground for nearly four hours. He has played and missed, been hit on the pad, survived a review. The crowd holds its breath.

Nothing happens for a moment. Then everything does.

That moment, stretched across time and earned through patience, is what Test cricket has always offered and what no three-hour format can replicate. It is also, despite two decades of confident predictions to the contrary, why Test cricket is not merely surviving. It is thriving.

The Forecast That Failed

When the Board of Control for Cricket in India launched the Indian Premier League in April 2008, the first edition drew 98.6 million television viewers in India alone. Franchise cricket, colourful and commercially explosive, had arrived. Within months, prominent administrators and former players were writing what amounted to Test cricket's death notice.

The logic appeared sound. Attention spans were shortening. Broadcasting revenues were moving toward T20. Young players were being signed by franchise leagues before playing a single first-class match. The economics of the sport were restructuring themselves around the shortest format.

Seventeen years later, the numbers tell a different story.

According to ICC data, global cricket viewership across all formats grew by more than 20 percent between 2016 and 2023. The 2023 Ashes series, five Tests played across England, drew a combined television audience exceeding 20 million in the United Kingdom. In India, the Border-Gavaskar Trophy remains one of the most-watched sporting events on the subcontinent. Its viewership figures routinely exceed those of domestic T20 competition.

The death that was so confidently forecast never arrived. What came instead was something more interesting and considerably more complex: a reinvention.

What T20 Actually Did to Test Cricket

Ben Stokes drives through the covers during the 2023 Ashes. His Bazball philosophy has redefined how Test cricket is played and watched.

The relationship between T20 and Test cricket is not one of predator and prey. It is closer to the relationship between an accelerant and a slow-burning fire. T20 did not extinguish the five-day format. It forced it to burn differently.

The transformation is most visible in batting philosophy. The generation of batsmen who came of age in the franchise era — including Rishabh Pant, Jos Buttler and Glenn Maxwell — were formed in conditions where scoring below a run per ball was considered underperformance. When these players entered the Test arena, they brought those instincts with them.

According to ESPNcricinfo, the global average Test match run rate climbed from approximately 3.1 runs per over in the early 2000s to over 3.5 runs per over by 2023. The proportion of Test matches producing a result rose from around 55 percent in the 2000s to over 70 percent by 2022-23. Test cricket is being played faster and producing more decisive results than at any point in its 147-year history.

The clearest expression of this shift has been England's Bazball philosophy. Since June 2022, England have overturned fourth-innings targets that previous generations would have considered beyond reach. They chased 277 at Headingley, 299 at Edgbaston and 296 at Auckland. Their win rate under Stokes and McCullum stood at approximately 75 percent through mid-2024.

The World Test Championship: Giving the Format a Narrative

For most of its history, Test cricket lacked the tournament structure that gives other sports their defining moments. The ICC World Test Championship, launched in the 2019-21 cycle, addressed this directly. Every Test series now carries points toward a championship table. The top two teams at the end of each two-year cycle meet in a final at Lord's.

The 2023 WTC Final between Australia and India at The Oval attracted a global audience of over 400 million viewers across broadcast and digital platforms, according to ICC figures. That makes it one of the most-watched cricket matches ever played.

The Demographic Question: Who Is Watching

The most persistent anxiety about Test cricket's future is demographic. If the next generation of cricket fans discovers the sport through the IPL or Big Bash or The Hundred, will they ever develop the patience for a format that unfolds across five days?

Research published by Wisden in 2023 found that among cricket fans aged 18 to 34 in India, England and Australia, more than 60 percent reported that their interest in Test cricket had grown over the previous five years. A significant proportion reported that their entry point into Test cricket had been a player they first encountered in a franchise T20 league.

Test cricket is no longer the entry point to the sport. It has become the destination.

The Economy of Meaning

The WTC Final 2023 at The Oval drew over 400 million viewers globally.

There is a quality Test cricket possesses that cannot be engineered into a shorter format: the accumulation of consequence. In a T20 match, a dropped catch is unfortunate. In a Test match, a dropped catch on the first morning can alter the entire character of a five-day contest.

T20 produces highlights. Test cricket produces history.

The Challenges That Remain Real

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that Test cricket faces genuine structural pressures. The first is the concentration of the format's commercial activity among a small number of nations. The second is player availability — as franchise T20 leagues have multiplied, national boards are increasingly unable to guarantee their strongest players for full Test schedules. The third is financial imbalance between formats.

But these are problems of administration and economics. They are not evidence that fans have stopped caring.

Still Supreme

Test cricket was never going to disappear. It is too old, too embedded in the culture of too many countries and too good for that. The format now knows its identity more precisely than at any point since Kerry Packer's World Series Cricket in the late 1970s.

T20 brought energy, revenue and new audiences to cricket. Those are genuine contributions. But the gift flowed in both directions. Test cricket gave T20 its standards: its sense of what excellence looks like and what it means to compete at the highest level of the sport.

Other formats entertain. Test cricket endures. And in enduring — through the franchise revolution and the demographic shifts and the economic pressures and the predictions of its imminent decline — it remains what it has always been.

Still, stubbornly, magnificently supreme.
